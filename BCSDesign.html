Design

Once you have a research question, hypothesis, and prediction, the next step is to develop the experimental design. In this section, we talk about the basics of experimental design, participants, and items.

Operationalizing a design

Experimental design is the means by which hypotheses are operationalized. In other words, experimental design is how you collect controlled observations (i.e., data) about the relationship among the variables of interest. Note that our hypotheses often involve predictors that are continuous variables. The predictor variable we've discussed on the previous page--the relative frequency of words--is an example of a continuous predictor. For the purpose of experimental designs, continuous predictors are often binned into a small number of bins (though this is not always desirable, it is a common convention and for the current purpose, it will make it easier to talk about the design options you have for your experiment). Binning turns continuous variables into categorical variables. For example, we might bin words based on their relative frequency into "high" and "low" frequency words.

There are also variables that are naturally considered categorical. For example, if we hypothesized that nouns are read more quickly than verbs, "noun" vs. "verb" would be a categorical predictor.

Just like predictors, outcomes can be continuous or categorical. Reading times are an example of a continous outcome. The accuracy of an answer to a comprehension question (which is either true or false) is an example of a categorical outcome.

Conditions

The first step in experimental design is to specify the conditions (e.g., test conditions and control conditions) that allow you to test your hypothesis. The conditions describe how we plan to manipulate our predictor variables. The goal of this manipulation is to test whether we will observe the change in the outcome variables predicted by our hypothesis.

To continue the example introduced above, consider that we plan to bin relative frequency into "high" and "low" frequency words. We would then design our experiment to have a high-frequency condition and a low-frequency condition. Our prediction would be that reading times will be faster in the high-frequency condition than in the low-frequency condition.

Without further additions, we would call this a by-2 design, because we have one predictor variable in two conditions and there are no other manipulations in the experiment. We will go through a more detailed example on the next page, but first we'll establish a little bit more terminology.

Participants

Another important design decision is what type of participant and how many participants your experiment should have.

Type of participants

The type of participant you should administer depends on the population your hypothesis is about. When we run participants, we assume that these participants are independently sampled from the population. This is a critical assumption, because we want the participants to be representative for the population. It is because of this assumption that we feel we can take the results of our experiments as evidence that speaks to the likelihood that our hypothesis holds for the population of interest. To continue the example from above, we might hypothesize that any healthy native speaker of English without reading problems reads high-frequency words more quickly than low-frequency words. In that case "healthy native speaker of English without reading problems" would be our population.

Note that we often make simplifying assumptions in selecting our participants. For example, college students are most likely not a representative example of the average "healthy native speaker of English without reading problems". Yet, it is commonly accepted to generalize from college student participants to the larger population of English speakers.

Number of participants

Think about it: Why aren't we just administering a single participant for our experiment? You probably have the intuition that this would be problematic. For one thing, there's only so much data we can get from one participant before that person gets tired or bored (and start providing less and less reliable data). But you probably also have the intuition that there's another reason beyond this. Even if we had thousands of data points from a single participants, we might wonder whether it is just something idiosyncratic about that participant that gives us those results. For example, what if that one participant--despite our best efforts to screen for it--has some form of dyslexia or other deficits that will affect how that participant reads? In that case, the participant would not be representative of our target population. This problem of representativeness is arguably particularly severe for experiments on aspects of higher-level cognition (including language), because there's more individual variation with regard to those aspects of cognition.

So, we typically want to administer quite a few participants, in order to feel confident that our results will generalize to the population of interest. But how many participants is enough? On the one hand, more participants cost more money and it can also take longer to recruit them, delaying the completion of your experiment. On the other hand, more participants make it more likely that we obtain reliable results.

One important distinction is between the overall number of participants you plan to administer and the number of successful participants you plan to administer. As we will discuss later, we sometimes have to exclude participants from the analysis. This could happen, for example, because there was a technical problem and no responses were recorded from them. Or the participant fails to meet a priori defined inclusion-criteria. It is the number of successful participants that determines how reliable your results will be.

How many participants we should administer in an experiment is often a complicated question. But there are rules of thumb. First, if you are conducting an experiment that is very similar to previous experiments, have a look at how many participants these experiments employed. Also ask yourself whether those experiments obtained reliable results: Were there results clearly significant (or just barely significant)? Were there several studies and they all replicated the significant result with approximately the same number of participants? If previous work suggests that the effect is somewhat unreliable (e.g., if previous work always reports significance values just below p<.05), you might want to plan to administer more participants than previous work.

Depending how comfortable you are with statistics, you can also run power simulations, in which you assess how likely you would be to find an effect under certain assumptions about the size of the hypothesized effect (based on previous experiments).

Regardless of how you decide how many participants to administer for your experiment, the most important thing is that you make this decision before you start collecting data. If you start to run you experiment and then decide later to add further participants, then you risk biasing your results. Simmons et all 2011(test)

(Critical) Items

In addition to participants, another important component of (language) experiments are items. To a first approximation, items are the stimuli we use in our experiment (we'll have more to say about that later). For the same reasons that we want to have multiple participants--our goal to be able to confidentially generalize from our sample to the population-- we also want several items in our experiment. We can think of stimuli in ways similar to participants. Any particular word, for example, might have individual properties beyond the ones we are interested in for our experiment and we want to be sure that whatever results we obtain hold beyond those individual properties.

Like with participants, we can look at previous experiments to see how many items our experiment should contain. A useful rule of thumb is also to have equally many items and participants.

Types of Design

There are many types of experimental designs. Choosing the most appropriate design depends on your hypotheses and the nature of the independent variables.

Within-Participant Designs

In a within-participant design, all predictor variables are manipulated within participants, so that every participant is exposed to every condition of the experiment.

Consider our example by-2 design described above, manipulating word frequency. As already described, there would be two conditions: high-frequency vs. low-frequency. For a within-participant design, each participant would respond to both high- and low- frequency words.

One of the benefits of a within-participant design is that you can measure the effect of experimental conditions on performance, while accounting for individual differences between participants (e.g., measuring the difference in reading times for high- and low-frequency words while accounting for the fact that people vary in their baseline reading speech).

Between Subjects Design

In a between-participant design, all predictor variables are manipulated between participant. That is, each participant is only exposed to a subset of the experimental conditions, possibly just one of condition.

For our example, one group of subjects would see only high-frequency words, whereas another group of subject would see only low-frequency words.

Note that, the same number of participants in a between-participant design provideless statistical power to detect an effect than in a within-participant design. There's an intuitive reason behind this: in a between-participant design, any difference that is caused by the design manipulation (e.g., high- vs. low-frequency words) now needs to have an effect over and above individual differences between participants. This means that the effect of the manipulation will be harder to detect.

However, a between-participant design is sometimes inevitable, for example, when your hypothesis (and thus the predictor variables) involves properties of the participants that vary across individuals (e.g., monolinguals vs. bilinguals; children vs. adults; fast readers vs. slow readers). In other cases, a within-participant design is logically possible, but a between-participant design is more appropriate because exposure to one condition is likely to have a systematic and undesirable influence on performance in another condition.

Mixed (between-and-within participant) design

In a mixed design, some independent variables are manipulated within participant and some variables are manipulated between participant.

Finally, note that design questions similar to those that we have discussed here for participants can also come up with regard to items. In that case, we talk off within-item, between-item, and mixed (item) designs.
