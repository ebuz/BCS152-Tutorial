Design

Once you have a research question and a hypothesis about how the independent variables of interest interact with the dependent variable, the next step is to develop the experimental design.

Experimental design is the means by which hypotheses are operationalized. In other words, experimental design is how you collect controlled observations (i.e., data) about the relationship among the variables of interest.

The first step in experimental design is to specify the conditions (e.g., test conditions and control conditions) that allow you to test your hypothesis. Consider the simple hypothesis that Variable A causes Outcome X. To confirm this cause-effect relationship, you must demonstrate that both of the following propositions are true:

  • (i) If variable A occurs, then outcome X occurs.
  • (ii) If variable A does not occur, then outcome X does not occur.

Thus, this simple hypothesis requires an experiment with two conditions: Condition A, in which variable A is presented; and Condition B in which variable A is not presented.

Types of Design

There are many types of experimental designs. Choosing the most appropriate design depends on your hypotheses and the nature of the independent variables.

Within Subjects Designs

In a within-subjects design, every subject is exposed to every condition of the experiment. For example, if your research question concerns how reading times for the final word of a sentence are affected by semantic predictability (i.e., whether the final word is predictable from the preceding context), you might design an experiment with two conditions: one condition involving high predictability sentences (e.g., I ate the apple), and the other condition involving low predictability sentences (e.g., I saw the apple). For a within-subjects design, each participant would respond to both high and low predictability sentences. One of the benefits of a within-subjects design is that you can measure the effect of experimental conditions on performance, while accounting for individual differences between participants (e.g., measuring the difference in reading times for high and low predictability sentences while accounting for the fact that people vary in their baseline reading speech).

Between Subjects Design

In a between-subjects design, the independent variables are manipulated between subjects. That is, each participant is only exposed to a specific subset of the experimental conditions. For example, one group of subjects is assigned to Condition A and another group of subjects is assigned to Condition B. A between subjects design is necessary when the independent variable involves properties of the participants that vary across individuals (e.g., monolinguals vs. bilinguals; children vs. adults; fast readers vs. slow readers). In some cases, a within-subjects design is logically possible, but a between subjects design is more appropriate because exposure to one condition is likely to have a systematic and undesirable influence on performance in another condition (e.g., ...).

Mixed (between-and-within subjects) design

In a mixed design, some independent variables are manipulated within subjects and some variables are manipulated between subjects. For example, consider that you want to investigate the effect of semantic predictability on reading times among both young and elderly adults. Age group is manipulated between subjects (because a given person can only fall into one of the relevant age categories). But both age groups are presented with high and low predictability sentences: i.e., semantic predictability is manipulated within subjects.
